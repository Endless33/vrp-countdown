What A Pilot Actually Looks Like

A common concern is:

"What happens if we apply and get accepted?"

This document exists to answer that question.

---

First

A pilot is not a purchase.

A pilot is not a deployment.

A pilot is not a commitment.

A pilot is an evaluation.

The objective is to determine whether VRP is relevant to a specific environment.

---

What The Pilot Is

A pilot is an opportunity to observe VRP behavior under conditions that matter to your organization.

Examples include:

- Session continuity
- Transport migration
- Failover behavior
- Recovery scenarios
- Replay containment
- Authority transitions
- Runtime continuity

The exact scope depends on the environment being evaluated.

---

What The Pilot Is Not

A pilot is not:

- Unrestricted source access
- Transfer of Protected Core logic
- Production deployment approval
- A purchase obligation

The purpose is evaluation.

Nothing more.

Nothing less.

---

Evaluation Process

A typical pilot may include:

1. Initial discussion

2. Evaluation goals

3. Scenario definition

4. Validation execution

5. Evidence review

6. Outcome assessment

The objective is to generate evidence.

Not assumptions.

---

What You Receive

Depending on the evaluation scope, participants may receive:

- Validation guidance
- Runtime observations
- Evidence reports
- Test results
- Scenario analysis
- Continuity findings

The objective is transparency of outcomes.

---

What Remains Protected

Protected Core remains protected.

Internal implementation details remain protected.

Proprietary mechanisms remain protected.

Pilot participation does not remove the Protected Core boundary.

---

Possible Outcomes

A pilot may conclude that:

- VRP is highly relevant
- VRP is partially relevant
- VRP is not currently relevant

All three outcomes are acceptable.

The purpose of a pilot is to discover reality.

Not to force adoption.

---

Risk

The primary risk of a pilot is time.

The purpose of the evaluation is to determine whether continuity-first networking provides meaningful value for the environment being examined.

---

Final Statement

A pilot is not a sales process.

A pilot is a validation process.

The objective is evidence.

The objective is understanding.

The objective is determining whether the problem VRP solves is a problem worth solving in your environment.
